What isCommunism?

Communism is an economic and political ideology developed in the 1840’s by two German socialist and philosophers named Karl Marx and Friedrich Engles. It is defined as “a political theory . . . advocating class war and leading to a society in which all property is publicly owned and each person works and is paid according to their abilities and needs.” The earliest example of communism being practised was in 1922, after Vladimir Lenin gained power as a result of a revolution in Russia during World War 1.

IntendedPrinciples

What were the intended principles of Communism in Russia during the 1900s?

The intended principles of communism during the 1900s were centred around the concept of a classless society, in which the means of production and distribution were collectively owned and controlled by the people. The ultimate goal of communism was to abolish private property and create a society in which everyone would have equal access to resources and the benefits of labour, basically trying to make everyone equal. The principles of communism also included the abolition of the state, and the elimination of wage labour and capital accumulation.

Intentions vsResult

What did it become (compare to its intended principles)

Communism was initially intended to create a classless society where resources were shared equally among the people, but in practice, it has often led to authoritarian regimes and human rights abuses. The Soviet Union, China, and Cuba, for example, implemented communist systems that resulted in the government controlling all aspects of society and the economy, with political opposition suppressed. Despite its ideals, communism has often failed to create the egalitarian society it aimed to achieve.

Reactions toCommunism

People have had diverse reactions to communism, depending on their cultural, social, and political backgrounds. Communist ideology gained support among workers and intellectuals who were dissatisfied with the capitalist system and sought greater equality and social justice. Many communist parties were established worldwide, with some coming to power in countries such as the Soviet Union, China, and Cuba. However, communism also faced opposition from those who saw it as a threat to their freedom and individual rights. The US, for example, viewed communism as a dangerous ideology that could lead to totalitarianism and the suppression of democracy. (McCarthyism and the Red Scare)
